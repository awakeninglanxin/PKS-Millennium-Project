Generation of new batik ﬂoral
patterns via chaotic modulation
and Julia-Mandelbrot fractal transformations
Michael Kopp1  and Inna Samuilik2,3*
1 Institute for Single Crystals, NAS Ukraine, Kharkiv, Ukraine, 2 Institute of Life Sciences and
Technologies, Daugavpils University, Daugavpils, Latvia, 3 Institute of Applied Mathematics, Riga Technical University, Riga, Latvia
This  paper  presents  a  novel  hierarchical  approach  for  generating  complex ﬂoral   patterns   through   sequential   application   of   chaotic   modulation   and fractal  transformations.  Classical  geometric  ﬂoral  patterns  are  ﬁrst  subjected to binary chaotic modulation using signals extracted from a four-dimensional memristor-based  hyperchaotic system,  introducing controlled  irregularity  into regular   structures.   Subsequently,   Julia   and   Mandelbrot   set-based   fractal transformations   embed   hierarchical   self-similarity   within   these   chaotically modulated conﬁgurations. The choice of the hyperchaotic system is motivated by its minimal complexity enabling practical analog and digital implementation, hyperchaotic  dynamics  providing  rich  modulation  signals,  and  capability  to generate  multi-scroll  attractors.  Analysis  demonstrates  that  global  geometric order  coexists  with  local  fractal  complexity  in  the  resulting  patterns,  with topological   features   and   symmetries   partially   preserved   throughout   the transformation  cascade.  This  synergistic  synthesis  of  chaotic  dynamics  and fractal geometry opens  new  possibilities for generating  patterns with tunable visual and topological characteristics applicable to digital art and textile design.

1  Introduction
The  use  of  Julia  and  Mandelbrot  sets  for  ﬂoral  pattern   generation  represents  a fascinating intersection of mathematical rigor and organic aesthetics. Fractals, inherently self-similar, perfectly mimic petal growth, vein branching, and spiral structures found in botany, such as phyllotaxis  [1, 2].  Fractal  geometry, also known as “nature’s geometry,” studies  irregular  geometric  objects  that,  despite  their  apparent  chaos,  possess  internal regularities  [3, 4]. Unlike traditional geometry, which operates with integer dimensions, fractal geometry works with non-integer dimensions, enabling more accurate descriptions of complex natural objects and creating patterns with controlled visual complexity.
The Mandelbrot and Julia sets are based on a simple iterative formula on the complex
plane: zn+1  = z + c. For the Mandelbrot set, the parameter c varies while the initial value
is ﬁxed (z0   = 0), with the set serving as a “map” of all possible shapes. For Julia sets, the parameter c is ﬁxed while the initial value z0  varies, with each point on the Mandelbrot map corresponding to a unique Julia fractal [5–7]. Of particular interest are areas near the boundary of the Mandelbrot set, where in zones of high turbulence, structures curl into

spirals and rosettes. By selecting the parameter c in certain regions, such  as  the “Seahorse  Valley,” one  can  obtain  forms  resembling orchids, succulents, or chrysanthemums [8].
Applying fractal geometry to the design of traditional patterns, such  as  batik,  signiﬁcantly  enriches  their  diversity  and  enables digital  preservation  of  cultural  heritage   [9].  Iterated  Function Systems (IFS) are among the most commonly used fractal methods, where   a  two-dimensional   IFS   is   a   ﬁnite   collection   of  aﬃne transformations  whose  solution  is  a  set  that  is  the  ﬁxed  point of Hutchinson’s  recursive  set  equation  [10, 11].  Various  studies demonstrate the eﬀectiveness of using fractal algorithms: L-systems are  actively  applied  for  generating  plant  patterns   due  to  their ability to mimic branching and growth [12,13]; escape-time fractals are  used  to  create  ornamental  designs   [14];  quaternion  fractals enable  generation  of three-dimensional  forms  resembling  exotic ﬂower buds  [15]. Modern approaches to batik pattern generation include the use of Fourier transformation  [16], stochastic fractals [17], and hybrid methods combining fractal geometry with cellular automata [18].
To  transform  abstract  mathematics  into  organic  patterns, specialized  techniques  are  employed,  such  as  orbit  traps,  where instead of simply checking whether a point escapes to inﬁnity, the proximity of iterations to  a  speciﬁc  geometric  ﬁgure  is  tracked, allowing mathematical lines to be transformed into textured objects resembling stamens or soft leaf edges [19]. Exponential smoothing (color cycling) using continuous coloring based on potential makes color transitions smooth, like gradients on living ﬂower petals [20]. Adding trigonometric functions by modifying the basic iterative formula to zn+1  = sin(zn)+ c or using polar coordinates introduces symmetry characteristic of ﬂowering plants into chaos [21].
The  central  question  of  this   study  concerns  the  behavior of fractal  transformations  when  applied  not  to  pristine  chaotic attractors   but    to   geometrically    preprocessed   structures    that have  already  undergone  chaotic  modulation.  This  methodology represents   a   hierarchical   complexity   augmentation   strategy: initially, chaotic dynamics inject irregularity into orderly geometric conﬁgurations;    subsequently,    fractal    transformations    embed hierarchical   self-similarity   within    these    irregular    structures. To   the  best   of  our   knowledge,   the   speciﬁc   combination   of chaotic  modulation  of  geometric  structures  followed  by  fractal transformations   has   not   been   systematically   explored   in   the literature.  Although  fractal  transformations  of chaotic  attractors and   chaotic   modulation   of   geometric    patterns    have    been investigated separately [22–25], their synthesis within an integrated framework presents  novel  opportunities  for  generating  complex patterns with controlled characteristics.
Of   particular    signiﬁcance    in    the    context    of    practical implementation   of   pattern   generation   systems   is   the   choice of  chaotic   system  for  modulating  geometric  structures.  From the  perspective  of  technical  feasibility,  the  most  attractive  are chaotic systems with a small number of nonlinear terms, which corresponds  to  a  minimal  number  of  electronic  components  in analog  circuits  and  reduces  computational  complexity  in  digital implementations [26, 27]. Hyperchaotic systems, possessing more than one positive Lyapunov exponent, demonstrate more complex dynamics  and  provide  greater  unpredictability  of  modulating signals compared to ordinary chaotic systems [28, 29]. In the last
decade, signiﬁcant interest has been generated by memristor-based chaotic  systems,  which  combine  the  advantages  of  nonlinear dynamics with the possibilities  of compact hardware  realization thanks to the unique properties of memristive elements [30, 31].
A    transformative    paradigm    has    emerged     through    the integration of dynamical systems with fractal geometry principles. The conceptual foundations originated from Julia’s investigations and  Mandelbrot’s  pioneering  discoveries  concerning  self-similar patterns in complex domains  [5, 6].  The  reverse  transformation methodology,  involving  the  determination  of  antecedent  points under    fractal    operations,    provides     unique    capabilities    for converting  established  chaotic trajectories  into  fractal-embedded conﬁgurations.  Bouallegue  et  al.   [22]  introduced  the  innovative concept  of  applying  reverse  Julia  transformations  to  dynamical systems,   establishing    the   “fractal    process”    methodology   for synthesizing  multi-scroll  and  multi-wing  patterns.  Their  binary computational  scheme  employs  inverse  square  root  operations on  bidimensional  sequences  extracted  from  chaotic  generators, systematically amplifying structural multiplicity through successive iterations [23, 32].
In  the  present  study,  a  four-dimensional  memristor-based hyperchaotic  system  described  in   [33]   is   used   as  the   source of  chaotic   modulation.  This   system  combines  the  advantages of    hyperchaotic     dynamics     with     memristive     nonlinearity, enabling the generation of multi-scroll attractors through fractal transformations. The system is characterized by a relatively simple structure,  making  it  attractive  for  both  analog  implementation based on operational ampliﬁers and memristor emulators, and for digital implementation on microcontrollers. An important aspect is the possibility of extracting two independent binary sequences from  diﬀerent  state  variables  of  the  system,  which  enables  the implementation of two-channel chaotic modulation of geometric parameters of ﬂoral patterns. The choice of this particular system is motivated by its demonstration of rich hyperchaotic dynamics with a minimal number of control parameters, the availability of detailed veriﬁcation through analog and digital implementation, and  the  ability  to  generate  diverse  multi-scroll  attractors  whose structure  can  be  used  to  encode  spatial  characteristics  of  the generated patterns [33].
Recent  advances  in  complex-variable  chaotic  models  have demonstrated   their    eﬀectiveness    for    generating   rich   fractal structures. In particular, one-dimensional complex-variable chaotic maps   [34]   and   multi-valued   models    [35]   provide   enhanced complexity through  complex-domain  dynamics,  while  hardware implementations  on  FPGA  platforms  [36]  conﬁrm  the  practical feasibility of such systems for real-time pattern generation. These developments  further  motivate  the  use  of  hyperchaotic  systems with hardware-oriented design, as adopted in the present work.
A central innovation of our approach lies in the introduction of a binary chaotic modulation scheme for classical ﬂoral patterns [37],  wherein  two  independent  chaotic  signals  simultaneously control  complementary  geometric  parameters,  thereby  injecting controlled irregularity into regular structures. We demonstrate that applying fractal transformations based  on  Julia  and  Mandelbrot sets  to  these  chaotically  modulated  patterns  creates  a  two-stage complexity enhancement process, generating structures that exhibit both  chaotic  variability  and  hierarchical  fractal  self-similarity.
Throughout  this  investigation,  we   analyze  the  preservation  of topological  features  and  symmetries  during  the  transformation cascade,   establishing  that   global   geometric   order   can   coexist with  local   fractal   complexity,   thus   opening   new   possibilities for   generating   patterns   with   tunable   visual    and   topological characteristics.
This  paper  is  organized  as  follows.  Section  2  introduces  a method  for  the   chaotic   modulation  of  classical  ﬂoral  patterns using    binary    sequences    derived     from    a    four-dimensional memristor-based  hyperchaotic  system.  Section  3  applies  fractal transformations based  on  Julia  sets  to these  modulated patterns and   analyzes   the   resulting   hierarchical   structures.   Section   4 speciﬁcally   examines   the   eﬀects    of   Mandelbrot-based   fractal transformations on the chaotic ﬂoral patterns, including analysis of  the   inﬂuence  of  transformation   parameters   on  topological properties  of  the  resulting  structures.  Section  5  describes  new methods for batik pattern generation based on chaotic modulation and fractal transformations, including point-wise modulation of ﬂoral  motifs  by  a  hyperchaotic  system,  and  the  application  of Julia set and Mandelbrot Multiscroll expansions to the resulting compositions.  The  Conclusion  summarizes  the  main  ﬁndings of the  study  and  outlines  directions  for  further  research  in  the synthesis of chaotic dynamics and fractal geometry for generating complex visual patterns.

Both signals are mapped continuously to the interval [0, 1] via min- max normalization (Equation 5), which constitutes the modulation law used throughout this work. The two free parameters  of the modulation  scheme  are the  amplitude  scale  amax   and  the  petal- frequency  scale   nmax.  Their   eﬀect  on  the  generated  pattern  is illustrated in Figure 2.  Varying  amax    ∈  (5, 10, 15)  while  holding nmax   = 8 scales the radial extent of the pattern without altering its topological structure. Conversely, varying nmax   ∈ (4, 8, 16) with ﬁxed amax  = 10 controls the maximum number of petals accessible to  the  chaotic  trajectory:  smaller  nmax   produces  sparser,  low- frequency petal distributions, while larger nmax  generates denser, higher-order structures. In all cases the global ﬂower-like topology is  preserved,  conﬁrming  that  the  choice  of  scaling  parameters aﬀects visual complexity but not the fundamental organization of the pattern.
To  further  verify  robustness,  three  normalization  strategies were  compared.  The  normalization  rule  (Equation 5)  maps  the raw chaotic signal x1 (t) continuously onto the interval  [0, 1],  so that  every value  in the  attractor’s  amplitude  range  contributes  a distinct modulation level (Strategy C). To clarify the role of this choice, we consider two alternative ways of using the same signal to  control  the  amplitude  a(X)  and  the  petal  frequency  n(Y)  in Equation 6.  In  the  ﬁrst  alternative   (Strategy  A),  the  amplitude takes  only  two  discrete  values:  a(X)    =   0  whenever  x1      <   0, and  a(X)    =   amax    whenever  x1       ≥   0.   This  corresponds  to tracking  the  sign  of  x1    and  reﬂects  the  zero-crossing  structure of  the   attractor.   In   the   second   alternative   (Strategy  B),  the
switching  level  is  set  to  the  mean  value  of x1    over  the  steady- state  trajectory,  so  that  the   signal  spends  approximately  equal time  above  and  below  the  switching  level.  The  amplitude  again takes  only  two  values,  0  or  amax.  All  three  variants  continuous normalization (Equation 5), sign-based switching, and mean-level switching are compared in Figure 3 for the ﬁrst cardioid pattern. The global ﬂower topology (radially distributed petals, approximate rotational  symmetry)  is  preserved  in  all  cases.  The  diﬀerences are purely quantitative: the continuous variant produces a dense, smoothly   graded   distribution   of  petal   sizes   and   frequencies, while   the   two   discrete   variants   generate   patterns   with   only two  distinct  amplitude  levels,  resulting  in  a  sparser  and  more regular  appearance.  The  continuous  normalization  (Equation 5) is therefore adopted throughout this work, as it exploits the full dynamical range of the hyperchaotic signal and produces the richest pattern complexity. This dual-channel approach assigns geometrically distinct roles to  each  modulating  variable:  in  Pattern  1,  X  governs  the  radial scale while  Y  controls  the  rotational  frequency;  in  Pattern  2,  X determines the mean ﬂower diameter while Y modulates the petal
depth; in  Pattern  3, X  and  Y  independently  drive  the  low-  and high-frequency undulations ofthe spiral arms. Since x1  and x2  are dynamically independent components of the hyperchaotic attractor conﬁrmed by the presence  of two  positive  Lyapunov  exponents the  two  channels  carry  uncorrelated  information,  ensuring  that amplitude  and  structural  variations  occur  simultaneously  and without synchronization. This produces richer and less repetitive patterns than single-variable modulation, in which all geometric parameters would covary along a single chaotic trajectory.

4.1  Mandelbrot transformation methods
We  apply  three  distinct  Mandelbrot-based  transformation algorithms to each ﬂoral pattern.
4.1.1  Method A: Mandelbrot orbit cloud
This method treats each point (xi , yi) of the ﬂoral pattern as a parameter ci    =  xnorm,i   + iynorm,i  in the Mandelbrot iteration. The  coordinates  are  normalized  to  an  interesting  region  of the Mandelbrot parameter space [−0.5, 0.1]×[−0.3, 0.3]i near the main
cardioid boundary. Starting from z0   = 0, we iterate z k+1   = z + ci
for up to Niter  = 15 steps, collecting all intermediate points (u, v) = (Re(z k), Im(z k)) until  |z k |  >  50. The iteration is performed until |z k | > resc. In contrast to the classical escape radius resc   = 2, larger thresholds (e.g., resc   = 30 and resc   = 50) are used here to allow longer trajectory evolution within a limited number of iterations, enhancing the resulting geometric structures. The union of these orbits forms  a  cloud-like  structure that  inherits properties from both the input pattern and Mandelbrot dynamics.
4.1.2  Method B: Mandelbrot multiscroll expansion
This iterative method combines forward Mandelbrot dynamics with  binary  branching.  Starting  with  the  normalized  pattern coordinates  as  initial  values  (u(0) , v(0)),  we  perform  D    =   2 expansion iterations. At each depth level, for every point (ui , vi ), we compute c = ui +ivi, iterate z = z2+c for K = 3 steps starting from z0  = 0, and generate two branches: (u, v) = (Re(z), Im(z)) and its reﬂection (−Re(z), −Im(z)). This produces 2D   = 4 distinct scroll- like  regions with  quadrant  symmetry,  analogous  to  the  classical Julia transformation but with diﬀerent internal dynamics governed by the forward Mandelbrot map.
4.1.3  Method C: Mandelbrot fractal web
This method explores the basin structure of the Mandelbrot dynamics by  computing  orbits  from  multiple  initial  conditions. For  each  pattern  point,  we  normalize  to  the  parameter  region near c  ≈  −0.75, then generate orbits from ﬁve diﬀerent starting points:  z0     ∈   {0, 0.1, −0.1, 0.1i, −0.1i}.  Each  orbit  is  iterated  up to  Niter       =   12  times  with  escape  threshold   |z|    >   30.   The resulting structure reveals how diﬀerent initial conditions in the complex plane converge or diverge under the same parameter value, creating a web-like pattern with radial connectivity and intricate ﬁlamentary structures.

method  A  for  organic,  high-density  structures;  method  B  for controlled multiscroll generation; and method C for revealing basin structures in parameter space.

5.4  Mandelbrot Multiscroll expansion of the chaotically modulated batik pattern
The Mandelbrot Multiscroll expansion is the ﬁnal and visually most   expressive   stage   of   the   proposed   pipeline.   Unlike   the Julia  transformation,  which  rearranges  individual  points  into  a circular  orbit  while  roughly  preserving  the  size  of  each  motif, the Multiscroll  algorithm  applies  a  global  conformal  remapping in  the  complex  plane  followed  by  repeated  mirror  branching, producing  a  qualitatively  new  spatial  organization  of the  entire batik composition.
5.4.1  Input preparation and global normalization
All nine point layers: the center-ﬂower leaves  (L1),  rounded petals   (L2),   dual-frequency   core    (L3),   four    side-ﬂower   rose instances   (L4),   four   olive-petal   instances   (L5),   and   the   four iterated butterﬂy sets  (L6–L9)  are  merged  into  a  single  array  of approximately 4 × 105  points at decimation step s = 2. Each point retains  an  integer  layer label l  ∈  {1, ... ,9} inherited  from  the original layer, propagated without change through all subsequent transform steps and used exclusively for color assignment.

5.5  Quantitative comparison of pattern transformations
To  provide  a  more  systematic  analysis  of  the   diﬀerences between regular, chaotically modulated, and fractally transformed patterns, we introduce the following quantitative metrics:
•  point count M;
•  radius of gyration Rg , deﬁned as the root mean square distance of all points from the center of mass;
•  box-counting fractal dimension Db.
The computed values for all stages are summarized in Table 2. The results reveal several clear trends.
The  regular  pattern  exhibits  the  largest  radius  of  gyration, reﬂecting a spatially distributed structure. The transition to chaotic modulation produces only a minor change in Rg , but signiﬁcantly increases  the  fractal  dimension  Db,  indicating  higher  geometric complexity.
The    Julia-based    transformations    lead    to    a    progressive spatial  compression,  as  evidenced  by  the   decreasing  Rg ,  while simultaneously   increasing   Db.    This    demonstrates    that    the pattern   becomes    more   compact    and    structurally   rich    with each iteration.
The  Multiscroll  transformation  shows  the  strongest  eﬀect, producing the smallest Rg  and the highest structural density.
Overall,  this  quantitative  analysis  clariﬁes  the  role  of  each module: chaotic dynamics enhances geometric complexity, while fractal transformations provide spatial compression and increased pattern density, thereby improving the expressive capabilities of the proposed method.
6  Conclusion
This paper proposes a novel approach to generating complex ﬂoral patterns through sequential chaotic modulation and fractal transformations.   The   central   innovation   lies   in   the   synergy between   a   four-dimensional    memristive   hyperchaotic   system and  fractal  mapping.   By  utilizing   a   seven-term  hyperchaotic system,   the    method    introduces    controlled    irregularity   into classical   geometric   structures   via    dual-channel    modulation. Simultaneously,    fractal     transformations    embed     hierarchical
self-similarity   into   the   resulting   conﬁgurations.   Applying   an inverse  Julia  map  to  these  modulated  patterns  preserves  the topological   features    and    global   n-fold    rotational    symmetry of   the    input.    Furthermore,    a    binary     iteration    algorithm with  exponential  point  density  growth   (M2       =    M1    ×  2N ) progressively   enhances    structural   multiplicity    to   create    ﬁne branching   details.   Among   the   investigated   Mandelbrot-based methods, the Orbit Cloud produces organic high-density textures, Multiscroll   Expansion   reorganizes    compositions   into   regular conﬁgurations, and the Fractal Web reveals basins of attraction as ﬁlamentary patterns.
The    approach    is     demonstrated    through    batik-inspired compositions  combining  iterated  butterﬂy  motifs  and  layered ﬂoral  elements.  These  patterns  exhibit  the  visual  hallmarks  of traditional   hand-drawn    batik,   including    organic    irregularity and  decorative  symmetry,  while  remaining  fully  reproducible. Due   to   its    multi-parametric   controllability,    this   pipeline   is highly  promising  for   digital  textile   design,  generative   art,  and visual cryptography.
